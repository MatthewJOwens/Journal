Three struggles, three successes, and three things I look forward to.

Week One

"The first three weeks are the hardest." They told us this on day one. And day two. And maybe day three. I just have to keep reminding myself about it. I've been struggling with getting frustrated at the sheer amount of things I don't know and am trying to learn all at once. I often feel like The Big Dumb and I know more of those days are coming. I do, however, feel like I'm middle of the pack. Neither the oldest nor the youngest. Neither the best nor the worst, but solidly average. That said, Friday made me feel a little different because when I struggled with something and tried to talk to my neighbors about it, none of them were ever as far as I was. I honestly got most of the assignment done in class. That's a good feeling, to feel like I actually know what I'm doing. Or that I'm at least not falling behind.

Something I struggled with this week was getting Font Awesome to actually work on my pages. I tried with the script given to me by Font Awesome in my e-mail when I gave it to them to get that very thing. I tried with the link from BalloonPop. I tried with both. Tried a new link. A different version. Multiple different versions, with and without the script. Just couldn't get it working. One of my seniors helped me get it working by first telling me to include both, then telling me that sometimes the fab is sometimes fab or fav or fas and I should try those, and when that didn't work showed me a VS Code extension that she uses to auto-generate the needed components. We tried that and it worked like a charm.

For something more mundane, something I had trouble with was during the Checkpoint, where we needed to have buttons that extended past the card they were a part of, or were offset in some way. We were given a hint that we would want to use position: absolute; so that's what I was focused on. (Throughout this assignment, I struggled with figuring out the difference between absolute, relative, static, fixed, initial, inherit, and unset.) I used absolute but the button was set off to the side. After investigating, I found that it was set to the right because the start of the button was centered, but I wanted it to be centered using the center of the button. In the end, I just measured the button's width using Chrome's dev tools and used CSS to change its position by half that many pixels.

The problem then was that the card itself was also supposed to be offset from the background, so there would be white-space under half of the card. No matter what I did, I couldn't find the answer. I asked Jim to see what direction I should be looking in and he said he thought he remembered using negative margins. I used a custom class to impose a negative margin on the card itself and it moved the height of the row up to where I wanted. This worked for the purposes of that row, but it came with its own challenge: I then needed that much more margin between that row and the next (where I put a row as a line break because I could find no other way to make one) so that the line break wouldn't be running behind the cards.

(Another note: I ended up using z-order with a custom CSS class in order to have the bottom of the cards to show on top of the picture from the next card in the mobile view because I couldn't get them to not overlap when viewing at a smaller resolution. I realize as I'm writing this that I could use mt-md-2 or something now that I've learned that margin can be made responsive as well, which I only found out later in the assignment when I asked that specific question of Darryl, our instructor.

As far as successes go, I have to say that the feeling I get from getting a piece of code to work, especially after some difficulty, is a great feeling. Just this sense of accomplishment that I have felt only rarely. I'm loving the challenges so far and it's feeling more and more like this is what I want to do with my life, something that I have been unsure of lately, especially when I'm feeling stupid because I can't get something right or when I can't remember things I just learned.

I look forward to getting into JavaScript. When doing the pre-coursework, I felt like the JavaScript was easier to understand than the HTML or CSS. Not that I feel it'll be an easy section, but I look forward to learning more about it. Also, it would be a nice feeling if I had a section where I felt like I had a natural aptitude for it. If it wasn't such a struggle to grasp the concepts and remember stuff, I think I would feel like I'm not forcing myself to learn a field of work that I'm not suited for.

I'm also looking forward to learning more about frameworks. JQuery, ReactJS, Popper, and the like. Tools that were built specifically to make this job easier to do. I'm excited to see in what ways they affect the dev process like Bootstrap does.

Finally, I'm looking forward to having my Checkpoint assignment reviewed and being told that I did a good job. I'm particularly interested in the feedback on what I could do better. The one thing I forsee hearing about is the buttons I mentioned earlier and maybe using the same method I used later in the project, just to be consistent in how I lay out my sites. Using two different methods to achieve similar results is probably frowned upon. But I'll let them tell me that.
